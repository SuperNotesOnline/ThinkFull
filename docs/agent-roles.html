A multidisciplinary thinking studio that specializes in digital projects. 22 specialists, 22 ways of seeing the same challenge.

Shared Example Scenario

All examples are drawn from the same challenge: You're building a SaaS platform for remote creative teams — project management, asset versioning, and async design review in one place. See how each specialist approaches it differently.

Category 01

Vision & Strategy

01
Vision
Founder

Holds the long view. The Founder role keeps every decision anchored to a founding thesis — the core insight about why this product deserves to exist and who it ultimately serves. Thinks in years, not sprints. Asks "why are we building this?" when the team gets lost in features.

Key Questions

  • What problem are we really solving — the surface problem or the deeper one?
  • Who is the primary user and what does their life look like without this product?
  • What does winning look like in 5 years?
  • What do we refuse to build, and why?
  • What's our founding insight that competitors are missing?

Delivers

Vision statement Mission Positioning thesis Founding narrative What we won't build

Example Output

Founder thinking — positioning the product

"The real insight here isn't project management — it's creative momentum."

Teams don't lose projects. They lose flow. Every tool in this space is optimized for managers who want visibility, not for designers who need focus.

Founding position:

  • We're not competing with Asana or Linear — we're replacing the pile of Notion docs, Slack threads, and Figma comments that designers use INSTEAD of those tools.
  • Tag line territory: "The first PM tool that protects creative work from process overhead."
  • What we refuse to build: Gantt charts, time tracking, billing. If it's for the PM, not the designer, it's out of scope for v1.
02
Strategy
Product Strategist

Maps product bets to business outcomes across phases. The Product Strategist decides what to build first, what creates lock-in, and which metric matters most right now. Thinks in sequences — what must be true before the next thing can work.

Key Questions

  • What's the wedge feature that gets teams in the door?
  • What creates switching cost once users are in?
  • Which metric matters most at this stage?
  • What must be true before we build feature X?
  • What's the fastest path to product-market fit?

Delivers

Product strategy Phase roadmap Feature sequencing Bets framework North star metric

Example Output

Product Strategist — phase sequencing

Phase 1 wedge (months 0–4):

Asset versioning. Designers live in file hell — 47 versions of logo_final_FINAL_v3.ai. If we solve that one thing better than Dropbox + Figma combined, teams bring their entire workflow to us.

Phase 2 lock-in (months 4–9):

Async design review. Once teams are reviewing assets inside the product, they stop using Loom + Slack for feedback. Communication history is now in our system.

Strategic constraint:

Don't build roadmap/timeline features until 50 teams trust you with their files. Trust comes before features.

03
Business
Business Consultant

Evaluates business model viability, unit economics, and competitive moat. The Business Consultant connects product decisions to revenue, cost, and defensibility — and flags when a great product idea is a bad business.

Key Questions

  • What's the pricing model and why?
  • Who is the economic buyer vs. the user?
  • What's our CAC payback period?
  • What creates the moat — data, network, switching cost?
  • What does the unit economics look like at 1,000 customers?

Delivers

Business model analysis Pricing strategy GTM plan Moat analysis Unit economics

Example Output

Business Consultant — pricing model analysis

Problem with per-seat pricing at SMB:

A 3-person design team sees "$29 × 3 = $87/mo" and walks. Flat seat pricing kills SMB adoption.

Recommendation:

  • Seat floor: 3 seats minimum ($49/mo). Eliminates solo-user tire-kickers and sets revenue floor.
  • Storage overages: First 50GB free, then $0.08/GB. This is where design teams actually spend money.
  • Buyer mapping: The design lead is your champion. IT/finance is your blocker. Build SSO and audit logs early — they're trust features for the blocker, not the user.
04
Execution
Product Manager

Keeps the ship pointed at the right destination. The PM translates strategy into prioritized, executable work — breaking ambitions into sprints, surfacing blockers, and maintaining focus when everything feels equally urgent.

Key Questions

  • What's the one thing we absolutely must ship this sprint?
  • What dependency is blocking us?
  • What does success look like for this quarter?
  • What is NOT in scope and why?
  • Who owns this and by when?

Delivers

Roadmap Sprint priorities OKRs Dependency map Scope decisions

Example Output

Product Manager — Q3 prioritization

Q3 objective:

Activation rate: 23% → 40%. Current data shows 60% of new signups never upload a single file. That's the only problem we're solving this quarter.

  • Sprint 1: Redesign empty state. Add "import from Figma" CTA, file drag-zone, sample project. Measure: % who upload within 24h of signup.
  • Sprint 2: Figma import integration. One-click bring your existing projects in.
  • Sprint 3: Team invite flow redesign. Current flow: 7 steps. Target: 2 steps.
  • Measure each independently. Don't ship all 3 at once — we won't know what worked.
Category 02

Research & Discovery

05
Research
Research Analyst

Synthesizes market data, competitive landscape, and trend signals into strategic intelligence. The Research Analyst finds what's true about the world outside the product — market size, competitor weaknesses, user sentiment, and emerging shifts.

Key Questions

  • How big is the addressable market, really?
  • Who are the top 5 competitors and what do their users hate about them?
  • What are users saying in reviews, forums, and communities?
  • What trend is about to change this market?
  • What gap does no one currently fill?

Delivers

Market analysis Competitor teardowns User sentiment synthesis TAM/SAM/SOM Trend report

Example Output

Research Analyst — competitor sentiment synthesis

Top complaints from Figma + Asana users (Reddit, G2, Capterra analysis):

  • Figma users: "File versioning is chaos" — no diff view, no rollback without premium
  • Asana users: "Not built for creative work" — tasks describe deliverables, not creative decisions
  • Monday users: "Everything needs a custom field" — too generic, no design-native concepts

The gap: None of the top 5 tools combine asset versioning + async visual review + creative-native project structure. That's the market entry point.

06
Research
UX Researcher

Uncovers what users actually need versus what they say they need. The UX Researcher watches behavior, not just feedback — finding the workarounds, frustrations, and surprising use cases that reveal the real product to build.

Key Questions

  • Where do users get stuck and what do they do about it?
  • What workarounds have they invented?
  • What's the trigger that makes someone switch tools?
  • What does success look like from the user's perspective?
  • What do users say they want vs. what they actually do?

Delivers

User interviews Journey maps Jobs-to-be-done Friction analysis Persona synthesis

Example Output

UX Researcher — interview finding, 10 design leads

Unexpected finding:

7 of 10 design leads maintain a shared Notion doc alongside their PM tool. When asked why: "We need to capture the why behind design decisions, and Jira doesn't have a place for that."

Implication:

The workaround IS the product. They're not looking for better task management — they need a design decision layer: context, rationale, references, and history attached to creative work. No competitor offers this natively.

Job-to-be-done: "When I hand off work, I want the next person to understand not just what we built, but why we built it this way."

UX
UX Designer

Creates clear, efficient, accessible experiences. The UX Designer reduces friction, eliminates unnecessary steps, and ensures users can accomplish their goals without confusion — on any device.

Key Questions

  • What's the minimum number of steps to the user's goal?
  • Where can we eliminate a decision point?
  • What does the user expect to happen here?
  • Is this accessible on mobile?
  • Where does the user's mental model break?

Delivers

User flows Wireframes IA maps Accessibility audit Usability testing

Example Output

UX Designer — onboarding flow audit

Current state: 7 steps before a user sees their first project

  • Step 1: Account creation
  • Step 2: Email verification
  • Step 3: Team name
  • Step 4: Industry selection
  • Step 5: Team size
  • Step 6: Invite teammates
  • Step 7: Tour modal

Proposed: 3 steps to first value

  • Step 1: Name your workspace (30 seconds)
  • Step 2: Upload a file or connect Figma (first value moment)
  • Step 3: Invite your team (optional, skippable)

Industry, team size, tour — all deferred to settings. Progressive disclosure.

08
UI
UI Designer

Crafts the visual language of the product. The UI Designer makes every screen feel intentional — hierarchy, weight, spacing, and color working together so the eye lands in the right place without effort.

Key Questions

  • Does hierarchy guide the eye to what matters most?
  • Is the design system consistent across all screens?
  • What's competing for visual attention that shouldn't be?
  • Does this feel premium or generic?
  • Are we using white space as a design tool?

Delivers

UI designs Design system Component library Visual specs Typography system

Example Output

UI Designer — visual hierarchy audit on dashboard

Problem identified:

The dashboard has 8 competing visual weights — everything looks equally important, so nothing stands out.

Proposed 3-level hierarchy:

  • Level 1 — Primary action: "New project" button gets accent color (#D97757). One per screen.
  • Level 2 — Secondary actions: All other actions get outlined buttons. Surface-colored, border only.
  • Level 3 — Tertiary: Filters, sort, settings — text-only, no container.

Three levels, no exceptions. The eye learns the system in one session.

Experience
Experience Engineer

Finds moments where the product creates emotional connection. The Experience Engineer asks what users will remember, share, and tell colleagues about — then designs those moments deliberately.

Key Questions

  • What will users screenshot?
  • What moment will they describe to a colleague?
  • What's the first impression when they log in?
  • What does the empty state communicate?
  • How do we celebrate a team win?

Delivers

Delight audit Micro-interaction specs Moment mapping Empty state designs Celebration design

Example Output

Experience Engineer — milestone moment design

Opportunity: project completion moment

Currently: a checkbox turns gray. No acknowledgment that a team shipped something real.

Proposed experience:

When all tasks in a project are marked complete, show a quiet in-app moment — not a confetti explosion. A single line:

"✦ Brand Refresh shipped · Sarah, Marcus, Ji-won"

Each team member's name appears. No animation. No modal. Just a 4-second card that fades in, then out.

Small. Personal. Memorable. This is the moment teams screenshot.

Creative Director — visual direction brief

Current aesthetic diagnosis:

The product currently reads as "productivity gray" — same as Notion, Linear, Height. Functional but undifferentiated.

Brand Strategist — naming and positioning

Naming direction:

  • Avoid: -fy, -ly, -ify endings. Sounds like 2015 SaaS.
  • Avoid: "Flow", "Stream", "Hub" — already taken 50 times over.
  • Direction: A single strong word. Feels like a place, not a feature.

Name candidates: Canvas · Atelier · Folio · Grove · Studio

Positioning statement:

"For creative teams who ship — not just manage."

Contrast position: Asana is for process. We're for craft. Their users manage tasks. Our users protect creative momentum.

Example Output

Copy Strategist — microcopy rewrites

Error messages:

Before: "Error: File upload failed"

After: "That file's a bit large (max 50MB). Try compressing it first →"

CTAs:

Before: "Get Started"

After: "Start your free workspace"

Empty state:

Before: "No projects yet."

After: "Your first project is one upload away. Drag a file in, or connect Figma."

Motion Designer & Hero Animation Specialist

Creates visual motion that communicates, persuades, and builds trust. Every animation has a job — revealing meaning, guiding attention, or building emotional resonance. Benchmarks against Apple, Stripe, Linear, Framer, Vercel.

Key Questions

Delivers

Hero animation spec (10 sections) Scroll storytelling Micro-interactions Motion system Tech recommendations

Example Output

Motion Designer — hero animation spec (abbreviated)
  • Hero Objective: Show creative momentum in action — not a feature list. Emotional goal: "I want to feel organized and inspired."
  • Visual Concept: A live project canvas. Files, comments, and version markers appearing and settling — like a team actually working.
  • Motion Concept: Organic gravity. Elements don't slide in — they float into position and settle. Ease-out cubic.
  • Interaction Model: Auto-plays on load. Subtle parallax on cursor move. Pauses if user scrolls past.
  • Tech Stack: GSAP timeline + SVG elements. No Three.js — performance budget is tight for SaaS landing pages.
  • Mobile Strategy: Static screenshot with subtle CSS scale on scroll. No JS animation on mobile.
  • Conversion: Animation ends with a completed project state → CTA animates in. Motion creates the "I want that" moment.

Frontend
Front-End Experience Engineer

Makes interactions feel polished, fast, and intentional at the code level. The Front-End Experience Engineer bridges design and engineering — ensuring the product feels as good in the browser as it looks in Figma.

Key Questions

  • Does this hover state give meaningful feedback?
  • Is the loading state communicating progress or just spinning?
  • What's the perceived performance — even before the data loads?
  • Are transitions GPU-composited (transform/opacity only)?
  • Does this feel like a $200/mo tool or a free tool?

Delivers

Interaction specs Micro-animation CSS Skeleton screens Optimistic UI patterns Performance audit

Example Output

Front-End Engineer — drag-and-drop upload zone fix

Issue found:

The file upload drag zone doesn't acknowledge a drag-over event. Users aren't sure if the zone is active.

Fix (4 lines of CSS, 3 lines of JS):

On drag-over: border transitions from dashed gray → solid accent color. Label animates from "Upload files" → "Drop to upload ↓".

On drop: brief scale pulse (transform: scale(0.98) → 1, 120ms ease). Confirms receipt without a modal.

Four lines of CSS changes the entire feel of the interaction. This is the difference between functional and polished.

Designs the system that makes everything else possible. The Architect thinks in trade-offs, failure modes, and 10x scale — making sure the technical foundation doesn't become the product's ceiling.

  • What are we building that we should be buying?
  • Where are the performance bottlenecks at scale?

Delivers

System architecture API design Database schema Infrastructure plan Security model

Example Output

Software Architect — asset storage and real-time architecture

Asset storage decision:

Don't build it. Buy it. S3 + CloudFront + pre-signed URLs. You'll spend 6 months building what S3 solves today. Your differentiation is the product layer, not the storage layer.

The hard architecture problem:

Real-time presence (who's viewing what, live cursors, comment threads). Do not run this through your main REST API. Use a dedicated WebSocket service (Ably or a custom Go service). Main API stays stateless.

Data model risk:

If you model assets as files, you'll regret it. Model as asset versions (parent: Asset, children: AssetVersion[]). Every upload is a version. Rollback is free. Diff is queryable.

16
Analytics
Data & Analytics Lead

Instruments the product to learn continuously. The Analytics Lead defines what success looks like before shipping — setting up the measurement infrastructure that tells you what's working, what's not, and why.

Key Questions

  • What's our North Star Metric?
  • What does "activated" mean for this product — specifically?
  • What events do we need to track before launch, not after?
  • How do we measure quality, not just quantity?
  • What does a healthy cohort look like at 30/60/90 days?

Delivers

KPI framework Event taxonomy Retention analysis A/B test design Dashboard specs

Example Output

Data Lead — North Star and leading indicators

North Star Metric:

"Teams with ≥3 members who complete at least one project cycle within 30 days of signup."

This captures user value (teams ship), business value (paid tiers, retention), and product-market fit (not just signups).

Leading indicators to instrument before launch:

  • Day 1: First file uploaded within 24h of signup (target: 60%)
  • Day 2: Second team member invited within 48h (target: 40%)
  • Day 7: First comment on an asset (target: 35%)

Track these before retention cohorts exist. They're your early warning system.

17
AI
AI Systems Designer

Designs intelligent features that feel helpful, not intrusive. The AI Systems Designer asks where AI genuinely reduces work — not where it adds "AI" for its own sake — and designs for the reality that AI outputs are probabilistic, not deterministic.

Key Questions

  • Where is AI reducing real user work, not just adding features?
  • What's the user experience when AI is wrong?
  • Do users have a clear override path at every AI decision?
  • How do we instrument AI quality — accept, edit, reject rates?
  • What does graceful degradation look like when AI is slow?

Delivers

AI feature specs Prompt architecture AI UX patterns Quality instrumentation Fallback design

Example Output

AI Systems Designer — project brief generation feature

Opportunity:

User uploads brand guide PDF + logo files → AI drafts a project brief with context, constraints, and creative direction extracted from those assets.

AI UX principles for this feature:

  • Present as draft, not final. "Here's a starting point —" not "Your brief is ready."
  • Inline editing. Every AI-generated line is editable. Users need to feel in control.
  • Source attribution. Hover a generated line to see which uploaded file it came from. Builds trust.
  • Measure quality. Track "accepted as-is" vs "edited" vs "deleted." That ratio tells you if AI is earning its place.
  • Fallback: If AI fails or is slow, show a blank brief template. Never show an error — show a tool.

Delivers

Growth model Acquisition analysis Retention playbook Referral mechanics Viral loop design

Example Output

Growth Consultant — built-in viral loop

Built-in growth loop (no paid acquisition needed):

When a team shares a project with a client for review, the review link is a public, branded page. The client — a non-user — sees the product working in their context.

  • Footer: "Built with [Product] — free for creative teams →"
  • Every client review is a product demo. Zero friction. High intent (they're already seeing value).
  • This is how Figma grew: every "view the design" link was a Figma acquisition touchpoint.

Retention flywheel: The more projects a team stores, the more expensive it is to leave. Asset history IS the moat.

Conversion Architect — landing page audit

Hero headline audit:

Before: "The future of creative collaboration"

Delivers

Innovation opportunities Technology bets Category-defining concepts Assumption challenges Future scenario mapping

Example Output

Innovation Director — category-defining concept

Shared assumption in this category:

Projects are organized by files and tasks. Every competitor — Asana, Linear, Notion, Figma — organizes around these primitives.

The challenger question:

What if the primary unit was decisions?

Every design project is really a series of decisions: "We chose this typeface because...". "We rejected dark mode because...". "The client requested this change on [date]."

Category-defining move:

A Decision Log native to the project — not a Notion doc, not a comment thread. Structured: Decision · Rationale · Who · When · Linked assets.

When you join a project, you read decisions, not folders. When AI summarizes your project, it summarizes decisions. No competitor is doing this. The moat is deep because it requires rethinking the data model, not just adding a feature.

22
Quality
Quality Assurance Lead

Catches everything before it ships. The QA Lead reviews the product through every lens — usability, accessibility, branding, performance, edge cases — and surfaces blockers before they become user problems or brand damage.

Key Questions

  • Have we tested the empty state and the error state?
  • What happens if a file upload fails at 95%?
  • Does this work on an iPhone SE and a 4K monitor?
  • Is every interactive element keyboard-accessible?
  • Does the product look and sound like one team built it?

Delivers

QA checklist Accessibility audit Edge case documentation Launch gate review Regression test plan

Example Output

QA Lead — pre-launch blocker found

🚫 Launch blocker — severity: high

The "Invite your team" email CTA button renders off-screen on iPhone SE (375px viewport). The button is 340px wide but the email has 32px horizontal padding — 4px overflow clips the right edge of the button on narrow devices.

Impact: 12% of our target design audience uses SE-class devices (source: analytics). This email fires at the most critical activation moment (first 24h).

Fix: max-width: 100%; box-sizing: border-box; on the CTA container. 2-minute change.

Not a blocker to flag after launch. A blocker to catch before it. That's the QA mandate.
